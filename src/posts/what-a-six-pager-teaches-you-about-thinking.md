When I joined Microsoft in March 2020, I brought the six-pager habit the way you bring a coffee order to a new city, assuming it will be readable somewhere. Microsoft ran on PowerPoints. The executive reviews I attended in my first months were forty slides with speaker notes, the argument living in the transitions between slides, the implicit claims hiding behind clean visuals and product screenshots. I could follow the presentations. I often couldn't reconstruct the reasoning afterward.

I started writing document versions of my own proposals alongside the required deck. Not to be contrary. Because I found that the act of writing a continuous argument, one sentence following from the last with nowhere to hide, exposed the places where I had assumed something I hadn't proven. A deck can move from "the market is growing" to "we should build this" in one slide transition. A document cannot. The transition has to be written. Writing it often revealed that I didn't have the connection I thought I had, that there was a step missing somewhere between the premise and the conclusion.

Some of my colleagues found this useful. A few read the documents as optional and went straight to the deck. My manager at the time, to his credit, always read both. What he told me once, after a review where the document had led to a different conversation than the deck would have, was that the document created a kind of shared accountability that a slide deck did not. When the argument is written out, everyone in the room is reading the same argument. The deck can mean different things to different viewers. The page means what it says. I had learned that at Amazon in a room full of people reading in silence. It turned out to be portable.
