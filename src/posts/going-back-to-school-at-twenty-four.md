The professor who taught the Algorithms course was already halfway through the syllabus when I realized that the students around me were twenty-one and asking questions I had trained myself out of asking. Not because the questions were bad. Because I had been in a job for two years and had developed the habit of not asking things I thought I should already know, which is a habit that takes effort to unlearn. I was sitting in the third row of a lecture hall in the Salvatori Computer Science Center, October 2016, still tired from a full day of working in the campus bookstore, feeling both too old and too behind simultaneously, which turned out to be a useful combination.

The difference was not really age, or not only age. It was money and attention. I was paying for this. I had taken a scholarship and used savings and taken a partial loan from my parents, and every lecture I half-slept through, my body still heavy from shelving and ringing up textbooks all afternoon, had a financial weight that some of my classmates, a few of whom had tuition covered by people who did not need to think about it, may not have felt the same way. I don't say this with any feeling about it. I say it because it changed how I sat in a chair. I took notes by hand in a blue Muji notebook that I still have somewhere, and I read the assigned papers before class, which was something I had not consistently done at Manipal.

There was also the working-person's lens that I could not turn off. When a professor described a scheduling algorithm, I kept thinking about the actual system I had built at Samsung and whether this theory would have changed my choices. Sometimes it would have. More often it wouldn't. Engineering education and engineering practice are running a conversation across a distance, and being on the practice side first made me more patient with the distance and more impatient with the parts of the curriculum that seemed to have forgotten the world outside the building. I did not say this out loud in class. I wrote it in the Muji notebook instead.
