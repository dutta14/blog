The honest version of why I left Microsoft in late 2023 is not the version I gave in exit interviews. The exit interview version was about growth and new challenges and wanting to experience a different kind of organization, all of which was true, but none of which was the first thing I thought about when I was lying awake at two in the morning trying to decide.

The first thing was simpler. I had been a manager for two years, and I had gotten good at it, and somewhere in getting good at it I had stopped feeling the particular kind of tired you feel when you are building something yourself. The tiredness of managing is real, but it is a different shape. I wanted to write code again. I wanted to be the person who had read the entire diff, who knew which line the bug was on, who could sit with a problem for three hours without a meeting interrupting it. Meta was offering that, in a domain I had never worked in. The combination was enough.

I told my team on a Thursday afternoon, in one-on-ones, individually, so nobody heard it secondhand. The last conversation went long and we talked about things that had nothing to do with the job change. Walking home that evening across the lake trail it was raining lightly, the kind of rain Seattle does in November that is less rain than moisture, and I felt the particular lightness of a decision that has already been made.
