I started at Amazon on a Tuesday in late June 2018, because they had moved my start date back two weeks for onboarding logistics and I had spent those two weeks in my Bellevue apartment reading every public Alexa paper I could find and telling myself this was preparation when it was mostly anxiety. The office was in the South Lake Union campus, Building Doppler, which I remember as a tall modern building with a coffee shop on the first floor and a lobby desk that scanned your badge with a slight delay that always felt like a test. I had a new badge that I kept checking my pocket for.

The first full-team meeting was on Thursday. My manager, Eric, ran it with a written agenda that he had sent the day before, and the first agenda item was a six-pager review. Someone put a printout on my desk and I read it while everyone else read it in silence, which I had been told to expect but which still felt strange, the room full of people reading, the only sound the ceiling ventilation and the occasional page turn. After fifteen minutes Eric asked if there were questions. There were many questions. The questions were pointed and unapologetic. I did not ask any questions in that first meeting. I took notes and tried to understand the velocity of the conversation.

By Friday I had an Amazon laptop, internal wiki access, and a list of eleven acronyms I needed to look up. I had been introduced to the leadership principles on day one of the general onboarding, printed on cards and also mounted in the elevator of the Day 1 building, and I had already started to understand that they functioned less as guidance and more as vocabulary, a shared language for saying that a decision was wrong in a way that the person who made it could hear. I had been competent at Samsung. I had been a grad student at USC. Being a junior engineer at Amazon in 2018 was a different kind of beginning, one where competence was assumed and the question was whether you could operate at a particular speed.
