The six-pager is six pages of dense prose, no bullets, no slide decks, no executive summary at the top because the whole document is its own executive summary. You write it before the meeting. Everyone in the meeting reads it at the meeting. Then you have the meeting. I had not encountered this before Amazon, and the first time I watched it happen, in August 2018 in a conference room called "Desk," I sat very still and tried to look like a person who found silence comfortable.

What the silence does is remove the performance of presentation from the evaluation of the idea. You can't charm your way through a six-pager. You can't have good slides and a confident voice and coast on the momentum of visual transitions. The writing is the argument, and if the argument has a gap in it, the gap is visible on the page without any ambient noise to cover it. I wrote my first six-pager in November 2018, for a feature I was proposing to the Alexa Android team, and I printed it and read it the night before and found three places where I had implied a conclusion without earning it. I rewrote those sections. The meeting went fine. The process had made the idea better before anyone else had a chance to.

I carried that practice to Microsoft, where documents were primarily PowerPoint decks, and I was sometimes the person who sent a Word document before a review and was asked what format this was. I was not trying to be difficult. I genuinely found that I thought better on a page than on a slide, and that I understood a proposal better when I had to write out every assumption rather than let an image carry the implied argument. That turns out to be a habit you develop, not a natural talent, and writing six-pagers for two years was where I developed it. The PR/FAQ was similar in spirit: start with the press release for the thing you want to build, work backward to the requirements. Imagining the announcement before the product exists clarifies what the product is supposed to be.
