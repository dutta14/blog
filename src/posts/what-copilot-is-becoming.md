In 2021, when I first told people I was building the AI assistant in Outlook, the response was a polite variation of "interesting, what does it do." In 2025, when I tell people I work on Copilot in Outlook, the response is a list of things they have tried, or things they have complaints about, or things they have heard a colleague tried. The product has a name now. Names change the relationship between a thing and the people who might use it.

What I remember from 2021 is a kind of quiet. The team was small, the feature was unannounced, the language model was impressive in ways we could barely explain to people outside the building and unreliable in ways we were working to fix. There was a particular pleasure in that, the pleasure of working on something that no one else knew about, before the vocabulary existed for it, before anyone had opinions about whether it was good. I don't want to oversell that pleasure. The quiet also meant we had no users, and no users means no feedback, and no feedback means you are building on instinct, which is exciting for a while and then becomes a different kind of loneliness.

Now there are users, many of them, and they have opinions, and the opinions come through telemetry and user research and the customer advisory boards and the posts on social media from someone who tried the thing and wanted to tell the world. The product is real in the way that things become real when enough people have a feeling about them. Being inside that, watching something go from a prototype in a small room to a feature that a person in Singapore uses to prepare for a Thursday afternoon meeting, is not something I have a clean sentence for. It happened over years and it happened gradually and one day the scale of it was just there, and I was still at the desk, still in the room where it started.
