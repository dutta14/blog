---
title: Letting someone go
date: March 8, 2022
excerpt: The conversation had been six weeks in preparation.
---

The conversation had been six weeks in preparation. HR was involved from the start, as they should be, and the documentation was careful and accurate and written in a register that felt distant from the actual situation, which was that I was going to tell a person they no longer had a job here, on a Thursday morning at nine o'clock, in a small Teams call with the HR business partner on the line.

I had written what I intended to say. I had practiced it, which sounds strange and is strange, talking to yourself in a living room the evening before. The point of the script was not to be robotic. It was to not be cruel. There is a particular cruelty in delivering this kind of news badly, in being vague when clarity is the only kindness available, in allowing any ambiguity about what is happening and what it means. I had seen managers be vague in these moments out of their own discomfort, and I had watched the person on the other end try to read through the vagueness, and I didn't want to do that.

The call was twelve minutes. I said what I had planned to say. The person asked two questions, both reasonable, both about process, and I answered them accurately. They said they needed to go. The call ended. The HR partner stayed on for three more minutes and ran through next steps in a tone that was professional and kind. I took a walk after. The day had more meetings. I went to all of them. What stays with you is not the twelve minutes. It's the specific face, even on a screen, of someone receiving news that changes their week and their month and possibly more, and the particular stillness that comes with it.
