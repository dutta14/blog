When you put on a VR headset, the familiar anchor points disappear. There is no keyboard. There is no fingerprint reader built into the desk. The face the camera sees is partially obscured by the device you're wearing. At Meta Reality Labs, the team I led was building authentication for Mixed Reality, and almost every assumption I had carried over from twelve years of building on phones and laptops turned out to be wrong in some specific way.

The problem that occupied us longest was deceptively simple: how do you prove you are you, in a space that has no fixed address and no stable physical interface? We were designing for a future where the room you're standing in could change, where the device might be shared, where the old models of identity assumed a kind of physical continuity that virtual environments quietly dissolve.

I don't think we solved it cleanly. I think we built something that worked well enough for where the technology was, and left doors open for where it was going. That is most of what engineering actually is.
