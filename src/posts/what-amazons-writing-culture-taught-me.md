title: What Amazon's writing culture taught me

The six-pager is a document you write instead of making slides. You print it, hand it to the people in the room, and the first fifteen minutes of the meeting are silent while everyone reads. There is something initially uncomfortable about this, the silence, the sound of paper, the awareness that everyone is reading at a different speed, that can feel theatrical but turns out to be one of the better ideas I have encountered in a corporate context.

The thing the six-pager forces is that you have to actually know what you think. Slides let you gesture at an idea, put a word on a background, let the presenter's energy carry the room. A document does not. A document requires sentences, and sentences require a subject and a verb and something being said, and the act of writing that sentence is frequently the act of discovering that you did not know what you thought until you wrote it down. I filled a lot of documents with sentences I had to immediately delete because once they were written I could see they were wrong.

I use this differently now, at Microsoft, where the culture is different and nobody expects a six-pager. But the habit of writing before the meeting, even just half a page, even just for myself, is one I have kept. It is not about the document. It is about finding out what you actually believe before you say it out loud to a room full of people who are deciding whether to trust you.
