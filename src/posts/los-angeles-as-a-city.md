The first thing you notice in Los Angeles, if you've spent your whole life in Indian cities, is the absence. No one is walking. The sidewalks exist in a kind of formal, unused sense, the way a formal dining room exists in a house where everyone eats in the kitchen. The streets are wide. The buildings are low. The light is different from any light I had known before, a flat, even, everywhere light that makes everything look slightly overexposed, and it takes a few months before you stop squinting and start to think it might be beautiful.

I never owned a car the whole time I lived there. I took the 28 bus on Wilshire, which runs every twelve minutes and takes forty minutes to go what a car does in nine, and I read papers on my phone and watched the city through the window. The people on the bus were mostly people who didn't have another option, and the people in the cars were mostly people who did, and there was a version of Los Angeles that existed entirely in the transit arteries and another version that existed entirely on the freeways and they barely touched each other. I lived in the first version for all of it, and I came to know the city the way you know it from a bus window, in long stretches, one stop at a time.

What I remember most, now, is the quality of a Sunday morning in Silver Lake, the coffee shops already full at nine, the cold juice places and the vintage stores and the bougainvillea falling over every white stucco wall. There was a loneliness to it that I felt most on weekends, when I had nowhere I needed to be and no one I had known for more than a year. The city was beautiful in a way that didn't care whether you found it beautiful. I think that's what people mean when they say it's a hard place to live. The loneliness is delivered in excellent light.
