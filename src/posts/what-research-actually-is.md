My second year at USC I worked in the lab of a professor who was studying natural language understanding for low-resource languages, which in practice meant that two PhD students and I were spending a lot of time with very small datasets trying to coax useful signal from noise. The lab was in a basement room in Salvatori Computer Science Center, four desks and a whiteboard that was always half-erased, and the PhD students were kind but distant in the way of people who are deep inside something and don't have much extra attention for the surface. I would come in at ten and leave at seven and go home feeling like I had been very busy doing something that had moved by about a quarter of an inch.

That is what research actually is, at the level I experienced it. You read for a long time. You run an experiment and it doesn't work. You read more and change one variable and run the experiment again and it works slightly better, by a margin that may or may not be statistically significant. You write a section of a paper describing what you did. Another person on the team reads it and asks why you didn't try the other thing, and you try the other thing, and it doesn't work better, and you write a section of the paper explaining why. I respect people who can sustain this over years. I found out I could not.

What I took from those months was less about the content and more about precision: the habit of being exact about what your evidence supports and what it does not, of not stating a claim larger than your data, of sitting with uncertainty long enough to name it accurately. Those habits came back to me later, in contexts that had nothing to do with academic papers, in the Amazon six-pagers and in the manager conversations where I had to be honest about what I knew and what I was guessing. The lab was a quiet place. I am glad I spent time in it. I am also glad I left.
