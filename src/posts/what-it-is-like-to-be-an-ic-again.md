The first week of code I wrote at Meta was an authentication middleware layer in Kotlin, and I wrote it alone, on a MacBook Pro that Meta had shipped to the house, sitting at the desk in the home office at about eleven in the morning on a Thursday in February 2024. I had been a manager for two years and four months. The experience of writing the code was strange in the way that returning to a city you lived in a decade ago is strange: familiar in the structure, different in the furniture.

What came back easily was the problem-solving part. Reading a design document, identifying the edge cases, writing the implementation and the tests and the code review responses. This was the same. What was different was the absence of the thing I had spent two and a half years doing: the weekly one-on-ones, the hiring loops, the performance calibrations, the particular kind of attention that a manager has to maintain over time for multiple people simultaneously. I had been holding all of that, and then I wasn't, and the absence was a kind of spaciousness that I had not expected to feel quite as much as I did.

The adjustment was real. I had opinions about the architecture that I had to learn to express as a peer rather than as someone whose role gave weight to opinions automatically. I had to rebuild credibility through code review feedback and design proposals, through the accumulated evidence of work, not through the authority that a manager's title carries quietly into every room. This was, in retrospect, exactly what I had wanted. The process of wanting it and the process of actually doing it were, as they usually are, not quite the same experience.
