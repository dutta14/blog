The Microsoft performance cycle ends in August, and for the three years I was a manager, I started writing reviews in late July, in the hour before the family calls and after the evening's meetings, sitting at the desk in the home office with the window dark and the document half-finished and the cursor blinking in a way that felt accusatory. The process was not the problem. The problem was the translation.

You observe someone over a year. You watch them solve a problem in a design review, under pressure, saying the right thing at the right time. You watch them miss a deadline and explain why and work late to recover. You see the email they sent at eleven at night that stopped a bug from shipping. You see the meeting where they were distracted and the project suffered for it. All of this lives in your memory as texture, as accumulated observation, and then the form asks you to convert it into a rating and a paragraph. The paragraph has to be true and specific and useful and also written in a language that is legible to a calibration committee that does not know this person.

What I got wrong the first time was being precise about the facts and imprecise about the impact. I could tell you what someone did. I had more trouble saying, in a sentence that someone else would read, what it meant for the team or the product or the person's own development. The form rewarded impact language, "this led to X," "this reduced Y by Z percent," and I had not always made the connection between the observation and the outcome explicit enough in my own notes to write it easily in July. I started keeping a document of weekly observations in September of 2022. The review I wrote the following August was the best one. The practice that made it better was ordinary: I had just been paying closer attention all year.
